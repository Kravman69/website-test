Important Safety Warnings

This product can significantly change your hormone balance. It is important to get blood work before, during, and after use.

Enclomiphene is a selective estrogen receptor modulator (SERM) that can increase luteinizing hormone (LH), follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH), and subsequently endogenous testosterone production by reducing estrogen-mediated negative feedback at the hypothalamus and pituitary.

Because it affects the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis, regular monitoring is essential.

Strongly recommended: Comprehensive bloodwork (Total Testosterone, Free Testosterone, LH, FSH, Estradiol [sensitive], SHBG, Hematocrit, CBC, and liver enzymes) before starting, periodically during use, and after discontinuation to assess recovery.

Research Use Information

Intended Research Target

This product is intended for research use in adult males who have demonstrated low LH and FSH levels on blood panels, consistent with secondary hypogonadism or similar research contexts where stimulation of the HPG axis is being studied.

Loading Dose vs Maintenance Dose

Unlike some other compounds, enclomiphene does not have a widely standardized “loading phase” in published research. Some researchers and users explore a higher initial dose (e.g., 25–50 mg daily) for the first 2–4 weeks, then reduce to a lower maintenance level based on bloodwork. Others use a consistent daily dose throughout. There is no universal consensus on this approach.

Lower Frequency Dosing (EOD / Every 3 Days)

Some research discussions and anecdotal reports mention using 12.5 mg every other day (EOD) or even every 3 days as a more conservative or longer-term maintenance approach. This is generally considered a lower total weekly exposure and is sometimes chosen by individuals sensitive to higher doses or seeking minimal effective amounts. Response varies significantly between individuals.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will this suppress my natural testosterone production?

Generally, no. Enclomiphene is designed to stimulate natural testosterone production by increasing LH and FSH through reduced estrogen feedback. It is often studied as a way to support or restore endogenous production rather than suppress it (unlike exogenous testosterone or anabolic steroids).

Will this increase my estrogen levels?

Effects on estrogen can vary. As a SERM, enclomiphene blocks estrogen receptors in certain tissues (including the hypothalamus/pituitary). However, because it can raise testosterone levels, some aromatization to estrogen may still occur. Bloodwork is the only reliable way to monitor actual estradiol levels. Some individuals manage estrogen through dose adjustment or other compounds in research settings.

What side effects are commonly discussed?

Side effects reported in research contexts and anecdotal discussions can include visual disturbances (similar to clomiphene, though often reported as less frequent with the trans-isomer), mood changes, emotional sensitivity, and symptoms related to hormonal fluctuations. Because it affects the HPG axis, individual responses vary widely. Regular bloodwork helps identify issues early.

Is post-cycle therapy (PCT) needed after using enclomiphene?

Because enclomiphene itself stimulates LH and FSH, many research contexts do not use traditional PCT afterward. However, some individuals still perform bloodwork after discontinuation to confirm HPG axis recovery. This is highly individual.

How long is enclomiphene typically studied or used?

Study durations vary. Some research protocols examine shorter periods (4–12 weeks), while others explore longer-term use. Duration should be determined by research goals and ongoing bloodwork results rather than a fixed timeline.
